# Plant Observations — Ridgeline Creek Riparian Zone

**Survey date:** 2024-05-10  
**Method:** Transect survey, 5 m belt  
**Surveyor:** Field Station Alpha  

---

## Canopy Species

### Red Alder (Alnus rubra)
Dominant canopy species in the lower riparian zone. Mature specimens 18–22 m height. Evidence of catkin fall across survey area. Excellent nitrogen-fixing associate; root nodules visible in eroded bank exposures. Alder-derived leaf litter provides detrital energy base for aquatic invertebrates.

### Black Cottonwood (Populus balsamifera trichocarpa)
Present at R-5 and R-7 stations as co-dominant. Cottonwood fluff abundant — peak seed dispersal observed 2024-05-08. Tall specimens 25+ m provide snag recruitment when they die. Look for pileated woodpecker excavations in standing dead cottonwood.

### Douglas-Fir (Pseudotsuga menziesii)
Dominant in upper riparian and upland transition. Recruitment of large woody debris to stream from blowdown events. Diameter at breast height (DBH) of the largest individuals recorded: 82, 91, 107 cm.

---

## Shrub Layer

### Native Willows (Salix spp.)
Scouler's willow (Salix scouleriana) and Sitka willow (Salix sitchensis) dominate bank zone. Extensive root systems stabilising eroded sections. Early leaf-out observed 2024-04-18; full leaf by 2024-05-01. When recording willow density: count stems per 5-m section, note whether browsed (beaver, deer, elk).

### Salmonberry (Rubus spectabilis)
Dense thickets in lower riparian. Peak flower at time of survey (magenta blossoms). Important nectar source for early-season pollinators and rufous hummingbird. Berries not yet ripe; revisit in late June for frugivore survey.

### Red Osier Dogwood (Cornus sericea)
Patchy but locally dense. White blossom clusters present. Berries in fall — important for migrating thrushes. Stems bright red in winter, useful for winter surveys.

### Hardhack / Steeplebush (Spiraea douglasii)
Dense stands at R-4 and R-6. Pink spike flower clusters. Indicator species for high groundwater; useful for mapping seep zones.

---

## Ground Cover

Sword fern (Polystichum munitum) widespread under alder canopy. Skunk cabbage (Symplocarpus foetidus) in seep areas near R-5-03; yellow spathe past peak. Stinging nettle (Urtica dioica) patches at disturbed areas near road crossing — wear protective gloves when sampling here.

---

## Invasive Species Notes

### Reed Canarygrass (Phalaris arundinacea)
Patches at R-5 and R-6 as noted in habitat assessment. Seed heads not yet formed at survey date. Flag with orange stakes for control team. Do not mow — stimulates growth. Recommended treatment: repeated cutting until rhizome exhausted, followed by native replanting.

### Himalayan Blackberry (Rubus armeniacus)
Sparse presence at road edge, R-4 station. Monitor for spread into riparian zone. Mechanical removal before fruiting; bag and remove — do not compost as seeds remain viable.

## Recording Protocol for Plant Surveys

When conducting a vegetation survey in riparian zones:
1. Establish transect parallel to stream at designated distance (bank edge, 5 m, 15 m)
2. Record all species rooted within 2.5 m of transect centreline
3. Estimate percent cover by species for each 10-m segment
4. Note height class (ground cover <0.5 m, shrub 0.5–5 m, canopy >5 m)
5. Record phenological stage (dormant, leafing, flowering, fruiting, seeding)
6. Flag invasives; do not record in same column as native species
7. Cross-reference with previous survey dates to detect change

Photograph representative individuals of each species at time of first detection. Use a scale bar or hand for size reference.

## Ecological Function Notes

The riparian plant community performs several critical functions that affect wildlife observation outcomes:

**Shade and thermal regulation:** Canopy closure keeps summer stream temperatures 3–5°C below open-channel conditions, supporting cold-water fish and invertebrates.

**Allochthonous inputs:** Leaf litter, terrestrial insects, and woody debris from riparian plants subsidise aquatic food webs. A healthy riparian buffer typically contributes 50–80% of energy inputs to small forested streams.

**Cover and nesting:** Willows and alders provide nesting substrate for Yellow Warbler and Common Yellowthroat. Dense salmonberry offers concealment for secretive species including Sora Rail.

**Bank stabilisation:** Root systems bind bank soils. Any disturbance removing vegetation should be flagged for restoration planning.
