# Macroinvertebrate Survey — Ridgeline Creek

**Method:** Kicknet sampling (500 µm mesh, D-frame net)  
**Survey block:** R-4, R-5, R-6  
**Season:** Spring (post-snowmelt, pre-flood pulse)  
**Surveyor:** Field Station Alpha  

---

## Purpose

Aquatic macroinvertebrates are primary bioindicators of stream health. The taxonomic composition, abundance, and diversity of benthic invertebrate communities integrate water quality, habitat structure, and disturbance history over timescales of weeks to months.

Key indicator taxa monitored in this drainage:
- **Stonefly larvae (Plecoptera):** Highly sensitive to low dissolved oxygen and fine sediment. Presence in moderate to high abundance indicates excellent water quality.
- **Mayfly larvae (Ephemeroptera):** Sensitive to organic enrichment and temperature stress. Diverse assemblages indicate clean water.
- **Caddisfly larvae (Trichoptera):** Moderate to high sensitivity depending on family. Case-building species (Limnephilidae) more tolerant than net-spinning species (Hydropsychidae).
- **Midges (Diptera: Chironomidae):** Tolerant; often dominant in impaired streams. High midge dominance relative to EPT taxa flags degraded conditions.
- **Aquatic worms (Oligochaeta):** Very tolerant; indicator of organic enrichment.

The **EPT Richness** (number of distinct Ephemeroptera + Plecoptera + Trichoptera taxa) is the primary summary metric for this survey block. Target: ≥15 EPT taxa.

---

## Methods

### Sample Collection
1. Place net downstream, open face upstream, with mesh resting on substrate
2. Disturb substrate within a 0.25 m² area immediately upstream by kicking and scrubbing for 45 seconds
3. Remove net; sort large debris into a tray; wash net into tray
4. Preserve sample in 95% ethanol in labelled vial
5. Replicate at three points per station: riffle, pool, bank

### Processing
- Sort in lab under 10× dissecting microscope
- Identify to Order for routine surveys; to Family for intensive sites
- Count all individuals per taxon
- Calculate EPT richness, EPT/total ratio, and Biotic Index

### Station R-4 (fast riffle, cobble substrate)

| Order | Family | Count | Sensitivity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Plecoptera | Chloroperlidae | 42 | Sensitive |
| Plecoptera | Perlidae | 18 | Sensitive |
| Ephemeroptera | Baetidae | 87 | Moderate |
| Ephemeroptera | Heptageniidae | 31 | Sensitive |
| Trichoptera | Hydropsychidae | 23 | Moderate |
| Trichoptera | Rhyacophilidae | 15 | Sensitive |
| Diptera | Chironomidae | 64 | Tolerant |
| Coleoptera | Elmidae | 11 | Moderate |

EPT richness: 6 families / 8 taxa (further identification in progress)
EPT/total: 216/291 = 74%
Assessment: **GOOD** — diverse EPT assemblage, low chironomid dominance

### Station R-5 (pool tail-out, mixed substrate)

Reduced EPT diversity compared to R-4, consistent with lower hydraulic stress and finer sediment at pool margins. Chironomid dominance increased to 42%. Oligochaetes present (n=7). EPT/total: 52%.

Assessment: **FAIR** — pool habitat typically yields lower EPT scores; compare to historical R-5 data.

### Station R-6 (disturbed bank near road crossing)

Significantly reduced stonefly diversity. Oligochaete abundance elevated (n=31). Chironomid dominance 61%.

Assessment: **POOR** — consistent with road runoff and bank disturbance observed at R-6. Recommend riparian buffer restoration and re-survey in 12 months.

## Field Notes — Observations During Sampling

While conducting kicknet sampling at R-4 riffle (12:15–13:45):
- American Dipper (Cinclus mexicanus) foraging throughout sampling period, actively using disturbed substrate — appeared to capitalise on invertebrates exposed by the kick-sample disturbance
- Two Pacific Giant Salamander (Dicamptodon tenebrosus) larvae found under sampled rocks; retained for measurement (SVL 12 cm, 8 cm) and returned to same location
- Tailed Frog (Ascaphus truei) tadpole in sample from R-4; confirm presence for water quality indicator database
- Stream temperature at R-4 riffle: 7.4°C; ideal range for cold-water EPT taxa

## Recording Protocol for Macroinvertebrate Surveys

1. Record GPS at each sample point before sampling begins
2. Photograph sample before and after sorting
3. Record substrate type, water depth, and velocity at each sample point
4. Note any unusual taxa or anomalies (parasitised individuals, deformities)
5. Preserve a voucher of any unidentified taxa for later expert review
6. Record air and water temperature at start and end of sampling session
7. Note weather conditions and any disturbance events in the past 72 hours
8. Log time from collection to preservation (should be < 2 hours)

Macroinvertebrate sample vials should be labelled: Site_Date_Replicate (e.g., R4_20240310_R1).
