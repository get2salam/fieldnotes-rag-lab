# Riparian Habitat Assessment — Ridgeline Creek

**Survey block:** R-4 through R-7  
**Dominant vegetation:** Red alder, black cottonwood, willow (Salix spp.)  
**Stream order:** 3rd order  
**Gradient:** 1.8% average, steeper in chute sections  

---

## Physical Character

Ridgeline Creek in this sector alternates between fast riffles and turbulent chutes separated by shallow pools. Substrate is cobble and gravel with occasional bedrock outcrops. Undercut banks are common on the outside of meanders and provide critical cover for salmonids and macroinvertebrates.

The floodplain is narrow (15–40 m) and partially forested with mature riparian trees. Canopy closure at 60–80%, providing summer shade that keeps water temperatures below thermal stress thresholds for cold-water fish species.

---

## Vegetation Zones

### Bank Zone (0–5 m from water)
Dominated by hardhack (Spiraea douglasii), red osier dogwood (Cornus sericea), and native willows. This zone provides bank stability, overhanging cover for fish, and terrestrial insect inputs to the stream.

### Lower Riparian Zone (5–20 m)
Mature red alder (Alnus rubra) with salmonberry (Rubus spectabilis) understory. Alder leaf litter is a major nutrient input; nitrogen fixation by root symbionts enriches the system.

### Upper Riparian Zone (20–40 m)
Transition to upland conifers (Douglas-fir, western red cedar). Large woody debris recruitment from this zone maintains pool structure and habitat complexity.

---

## Microhabitats and Their Wildlife Significance

**Emergent rocks in riffles:** Critical foraging substrate for dippers and certain warblers. Also basking sites for water snakes and turtles where present. Note whether birds perch on rocks above or below the waterline.

**Riffles and fast water:** Highly oxygenated zones supporting dense macroinvertebrate communities — stoneflies, mayflies, caddisflies. Primary foraging area for dippers and harlequin ducks.

**Turbulent chutes:** Extreme velocity habitat used by specialist species only. Harlequin ducks are characteristic; avoid disturbance in these sections during breeding season.

**Pool tail-outs:** Transitional zones where current slows; preferred by mergansers and kingfishers for fish-watching.

**Undercut banks:** Record dimensions (depth, width), substrate type, and any signs of use by mink, otters, or water voles.

**Willow fringe:** Highest insect diversity; attracts breeding warblers, flycatchers, and swallows.

---

## Observation Notes — Habitat Quality

When recording microhabitat context during wildlife observations:
- Identify which of the above zones the animal occupied
- Note whether the animal was at the water surface, below it, or in riparian vegetation
- Record water velocity at the observation point if measurable (fast, moderate, slow)
- Note bank exposure: shaded vs open
- Record whether observation was from above or at bank level (affects detection probability)

---

## Disturbance and Threats

Human disturbance is elevated at road crossing R-4. Off-road vehicle use on the bank east of the culvert has caused soil compaction and vegetation removal. Recommend fencing with native planting buffer.

Invasive Reed Canarygrass (Phalaris arundinacea) present in patches at R-5 and R-6. Treat before seed set; do not move soil between sites.

Deadfall from a wind event last winter has partially blocked the channel at R-6-02, creating a new pool. Monitor for fish use; do not remove until survey confirms no critical habitat.

## Key Measurements to Record at Each Station

| Parameter | Method | Unit |
|---|---|---|
| Channel width | Tape measure at bankfull | m |
| Water depth | Wading rod at thalweg | m |
| Water temperature | Digital probe | °C |
| Canopy closure | Densiometer | % |
| Dominant substrate | Visual estimate of D50 | mm |
| Turbidity | Secchi depth or visual | NTU or class |

Record GPS at each measurement point. All readings should be taken at the same point relative to the channel cross-section for repeatability.
