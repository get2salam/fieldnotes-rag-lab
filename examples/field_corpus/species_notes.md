# Species Notes — Riparian Vertebrates

**Compiled for:** Ridgeline Creek Survey Block  
**Taxonomic scope:** Fish, amphibians, reptiles, mammals  
**Last updated:** 2024-05-15  

---

## Fish

### Coastal Cutthroat Trout (Oncorhynchus clarkii clarkii)
**Status:** Resident and sea-run forms present  
**Habitat:** Pools, undercut banks, log jam pools; avoids high-velocity chutes  
**Observation tips:** Most active at dawn and dusk when feeding at surface. Look for rise rings in pools. Juveniles visible in shallower riffles.  
**Notes:** Do not approach within 10 m of a pool where fish are visible until survey is complete — they spook readily. Use polarised glasses and position yourself with sun at your back.  

### Coho Salmon (Oncorhynchus kisutch)
**Status:** Seasonal — adult migration typically October–December; juveniles year-round  
**Habitat:** Adults hold in pools below barriers; spawning redds in gravel of moderate-velocity reaches  
**Observation tips:** Redd count method: locate disturbed gravel patches (clean cobble surrounded by silt). Record location, size (length × width cm), and count of adults holding upstream.  
**Notes:** Spawning activity is a protected event; no wading within 30 m of active redds.  

### Pacific Lamprey (Entosphenus tridentatus)
**Status:** Declining; record any sightings  
**Habitat:** Adults migrate upstream May–August; ammocoetes (larvae) in fine sediment banks  
**Observation tips:** Adults are eel-like, 40–70 cm, bluish-silver. May attach to rocks or other fish. Larvae found when sediment is disturbed but do not deliberately excavate for them.

## Amphibians

### Pacific Giant Salamander (Dicamptodon tenebrosus)
**Status:** Common locally  
**Habitat:** Under rocks in cold, fast-moving streams; also in moist forest floor  
**Observation tips:** Survey by turning streamside rocks >15 cm diameter. Replace rock exactly as found. Record: body length (snout to vent), coloration, life stage (larval vs transformed).  
**Notes:** Larvae are neotenic at many sites — may reach 25–30 cm without metamorphosing. Do not confuse with ensatina salamander, which is terrestrial.  

### Tailed Frog (Ascaphus truei)
**Status:** Cold-water specialist; presence indicates high water quality  
**Habitat:** Fast, cold, shaded streams; under rocks in riffles  
**Observation tips:** Tadpoles distinctive — large oral disc for adhering to rocks in fast flow. Adults more difficult; mostly nocturnal. Internal fertilisation (hence "tail" — it is a copulatory organ in males).  
**Notes:** Highly sensitive to sedimentation. Absence from previously occupied sites is a serious water quality flag.  

### Western Toad (Anaxyrus boreas)
**Status:** Common but declining regionally  
**Habitat:** Breeds in still or slow pools; adults range widely in upland habitats  
**Observation tips:** Breeding aggregations at R-7-pool in April–May. Mass migrations of metamorphs in June — watch for tiny toads on trails after rain.  
**Notes:** Handle minimally and with wet hands; skin absorbs contaminants. Chytrid fungus (Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis) present in region — disinfect footwear between sites.

## Reptiles

### Western Terrestrial Garter Snake (Thamnophis elegans)
**Status:** Common  
**Habitat:** Streamside vegetation and rock outcrops; avoids open water  
**Observation tips:** Often basking on warm rocks at midday. Key field marks: three cream to yellow stripes on dark body, spotted pattern between stripes. Tongue flickering is prey detection, not aggression.  
**Notes:** Non-venomous. May musk if handled — wash hands. Observe without capture when possible.  

### Common Gartersnake (Thamnophis sirtalis)
**Status:** Common; more aquatic than T. elegans  
**Habitat:** Water edges, swimming in pools; sometimes foraging under water  
**Observation tips:** Distinguished from T. elegans by more vivid stripe pattern and less distinct spots. Highly variable colouration; confirm stripe position (top and sides) for ID.

## Mammals

### American Mink (Neovison vison)
**Status:** Resident; signs common  
**Habitat:** Bank burrows, under roots at water edge  
**Observation tips:** Spraint (scat) on prominent rocks at water's edge — dark, twisted, fish-smelling. Footprints in mud: 5-toed, with slight webbing. May be seen hunting along bank at dawn/dusk.  

### North American Otter (Lontra canadensis)
**Status:** Present; occasional records  
**Habitat:** Pools, slow reaches; uses deep pools for fish hunting  
**Observation tips:** Slides on muddy or snowy banks — wide body impression. Spraint large and fishy; often with fish scales. Rolls in vegetation after eating. Very shy — approach slowly.  
**Notes:** Record all otter sightings with confidence level (definite, probable, possible) and evidence type.  

### Beaver (Castor canadensis)
**Status:** No active dams in R-4–R-7; evidence of historical activity at R-6  
**Observation tips:** Gnawed stumps (angled cut, tooth marks 3–5 mm wide); tree drag marks to water; lodge or dam structure.  
**Notes:** If active beaver activity is found, flag immediately — their engineering significantly alters hydrology. Report to project lead before next survey visit.

## Sampling and Ethics

For all vertebrate surveys:
1. Minimise handling; use catch-and-release only with appropriate permits
2. Disinfect sampling equipment between sites (10% bleach solution or 70% ethanol)
3. Do not record exact GPS coordinates of sensitive species (cutthroat trout, lamprey, tailed frog) in field-shareable data; use grid reference only
4. Report any dead animals with photos; preserve if decomposition recent and carcass intact
5. Do not collect tissue samples unless authorised by research permit
